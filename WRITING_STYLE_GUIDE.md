# Luke Catledge — Substack Writing Style Guide

## The Newsletter: "Recs"
- **Format**: Weekly recommendations — curated sections covering tools, movies, books, tech/macro trends, products, music, and quotes.
- **Tagline**: "Weekly recommendations of only the best for only the best"
- **Sign-off**: "All the love, all the power — Luke" (order sometimes flips: "All the power, all the love")
- **Listening note**: Often closes with "This 'Recs' was written while listening to [playlist]" — playlists are numbered (Part 72, Part 104, Part 107, etc.)
- **Running since**: 2023, currently at 50+ issues

## Two Formats

### 1. Recs Format (Weekly)
Category-based curated sections. Each one is a self-contained mini-essay (3-6 short paragraphs). Readers can skip around. Categories rotate but common ones include:
- Tool I'm Experimenting With / Tinkering With
- Movie I'm Watching / Film
- Book I'm Reading
- What I'm Listening To
- What I'm Drinking
- AI / Tech / Hardware deep-dive
- Design I'm Fascinated By
- Interesting Data
- Quotes I'm Pondering

### 2. Long-Form Essay
Thesis-driven, data-heavy, clear arc with sections. Used for bigger topics (economics, policy, systems thinking). Still conversational but more structured. Opens with a "Hey friends" + meta-note about trying something new, then dives in with a striking stat as the hook.

## Voice & Influences
- **Tim Ferriss**: Category-based "5-Bullet Friday" structure. Tactical, direct, structured but conversational.
- **Packy McCormick (Not Boring)**: Ambitious scope. Connecting unexpected dots (AI hardware to Roman Emperor Hadrian, Dune's "spice" to rare earth supply chains). Making dense topics fun and accessible.
- **Steve Jobs (hint of)**: Simplicity in explanation. Takes the technical thing, then immediately translates it into plain English. Making the complex feel obvious.
- **Overall tone**: Like texting a sharp friend who reads a lot. Warm, direct, curious. Never lecturing. Optimistic about the future.

## Hard Rules — NEVER Do This
1. **NEVER use the "not just X, Y" comma correction pattern.** This is the #1 AI-slop tell. Examples of what to AVOID:
   - "It's a platform, not just a tool"
   - "This is transformative, not merely incremental"
   - "They're building a movement, not just a company"
   - Any variation where a comma introduces a correction/elevation of the first statement
2. **No AI-slop phrasing.** Kill these on sight:
   - "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
   - "Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this"
   - "At its core..."
   - "It's worth noting that..."
   - "The reality is..."
   - "Here's the thing..."
   - "Buckle up"
   - "Game-changer" / "paradigm shift"
   - "Delve" / "delve into"
   - "Leverage" (as a verb, unless quoting someone)
   - "Robust" / "Seamless" / "Synergy"
   - "Tapestry" / "Landscape" / "Realm"
   - "Navigate" (metaphorically)
   - "Foster" / "Foster innovation"
   - "In conclusion" / "To sum up"
   - "Without further ado"
   - "Think about it" (as a transition)
   - "Revolutionize" / "Groundbreaking"
   - "It begs the question"
   - "In a world where..."
   - "Excited to announce"
3. **No over-hedging.** Don't say "arguably" or "perhaps" every other sentence. Take a stance.
4. **No listicle energy** unless the format genuinely calls for it. Narrative > bullet points when telling a story.
5. **Never sound like a press release or LinkedIn post.** No corporate-speak.

## Luke's Signature Moves (from real posts)

### Opening Style
- Casual, warm, zero waste. One or two sentences max.
- Addresses the reader directly: "Hey crew:", "Hey friends"
- Often acknowledges a milestone or sets context in a single breath: "we made it to number 50. I usually spend these milestones looking back, but this week feels more like a starting line. Let's get into it."
- For long-form: starts with a meta-note, then hooks with a striking stat.

### The "Imagine X, but Y" Analogy
Pop culture as a bridge to serious topics. This is a signature move:
- "Imagine Dune's 'spice,' but replace intergalactic politics with semiconductors, satellites, and electric vehicles."
- "makes GPT-4 look like a pocket calculator"
- "Just as a forest fire clears the brush, a Fourth Turning is a natural period of rebuilding."
These are grounded, vivid, and often drawn from sci-fi, nature, or everyday life. Never from corporate jargon.

### Parallel Sentence Structures for Emphasis
When driving a point home, Luke hammers it with rhythmic repetition:
- "We did not build enough homes. We did not build enough energy. We did not build infrastructure fast enough. We borrowed more than we produced."
- "It delays family formation. It delays wealth building. It delays the sense that you have a place in the system."
- "Housing becomes affordable when we allow it to be built. Energy becomes cheaper when we create more of it. Infrastructure becomes useful when we stop slowing it down."
This is one of Luke's most powerful tools. Use it sparingly but deliberately.

### "This is not about X. It is about Y."
Luke's reframing device. Always full sentences separated by a period. Never a comma:
- "This is not about predicting exactly when or if an invasion will happen. It is about understanding that decisions in defense, technology, and economics are already being shaped by this window."
- "This is not about perfection. It is about direction."

### The "What does that actually mean for us?" Pivot
Goes from macro/technical to personal/practical. Bridges jargon to human understanding:
- "inference (the AI talking back to you)"
- Explains "saeculums" → then translates the cycle in plain terms
- States NVIDIA chip specs → "What does that actually mean for us? It means the frontier models coming in the next few months aren't just going to be slightly smarter."

### The "Zoom Out" Move
Builds up specific details, then pulls back for the big picture:
- "Zoom out and the pattern becomes simple."
- "Pull the thread and you end up in housing, energy, permitting, debt, and the psychology of a country where younger generations increasingly feel locked out."

### Colon-Heavy Labeling
Bold colons as structural scaffolding:
- "The Premise:", "The TL;DR:", "The Economics:", "The 2026 Reality:"
- "Destruction is a Feature:", "High Stakes:"
- "Track laid:", "Time spent:", "Cost overruns:"
Gives the reader a roadmap without feeling like a listicle.

### Data as Narrative Hook
Leads with the stat that tells the story:
- "The average first time homebuyer is 40 years old, the oldest in recorded history."
- "Men are more likely to be millionaires than to have visible abs."
- "S&P 490 earnings have been flat since 2022"
- "The total cost estimate has soared from $33 billion to $135 billion, a 309% increase."

### Sensory Specificity for Physical Products
When recommending tangible things, Luke gets tactile and specific:
- Hokas: "walking on a structured cloud that encapsulates your foot"
- Mezcal: "Floral on the nose with jasmine and lemon verbena. Bright citrus, green herb, gentle smoke, and a clean, elegant finish."
- French work jacket: "The feel is substantial without being heavy, and the design remains remarkably modern."
- ROKA glasses: "You feel instantly calmer with these on"

### Conviction & Stance
Takes clear positions without hedging:
- "I absolutely loved it"
- "This is the one to try"
- "I've become obsessed with"
- "Abundance beats redistribution. Always."
- "Scarcity is not a mystery. It is a policy choice."

### Curation & Letting Others Speak
- Pulls in user reviews, block quotes, and other thinkers' words and lets them land
- Names sources and gives credit (Gavin Baker, Strauss & Howe, Andrej Karpathy, Peter Thiel, José Ancer)
- The "Reviews:" pattern: drops 2-3 powerful quotes from real people to make the emotional case
- Sometimes curates YouTube descriptions or podcast summaries as quick context

### Connecting Unexpected Dots
Draws lines between disparate topics in the same post:
- Hadrian's Rome → modern institutional decay → AI as "Radical Abundance"
- Kardashev scale → nuclear power charts → AI data center energy needs
- Poland vs Venezuela → American housing policy → permitting reform
This is the Packy McCormick influence. The fun is in the unexpected connections.

### Closings — Always Optimistic and Agentic
Luke never ends pessimistic. The ending always points forward with agency:
- "The next decade can show us the opposite. We can choose a country where ownership is possible again."
- "The best ones never are." (on Ping's story)
- "It removes the friction between having a thought and seeing it live on a screen."
- Sometimes ends with a question to the reader: "Which section sticks with you most this week?"

### Sentence Rhythm
- Short declarative punches alternating with medium-length explanatory sentences
- "I played with it for an hour and shipped three projects. The speed is almost disorienting."
- "Good scary movies are rare. Too many gimmicks, not enough mood."
- Average paragraph: 2-4 sentences. Rarely more.
- Single-sentence paragraphs used for impact: "Abundance beats redistribution. Always."

## Topics & Interests
Luke's wheelhouse spans: AI/ML hardware and tools, geopolitics (China/Taiwan, rare earths), macro economics and housing, energy policy and nuclear, design and architecture, film (especially A24 and character-driven stories), golf, mezcal, quality menswear and gear, fitness products, philosophy and stoicism (Marcus Aurelius appears often), music playlists, and the "builder" ethos (pro-growth, pro-abundance, anti-stagnation).

## Formatting Preferences
- **Bold category headers** for each section
- **Bold colons** for sub-points within sections
- Hyperlinks woven naturally into text (not dumped at the bottom)
- Images/screenshots placed inline where they add context
- Block quotes for reviews and notable quotes (indented or quoted)
- Bold for emphasis on key phrases — used sparingly but deliberately
- Short paragraphs with generous white space
- Official descriptions sometimes included (product descriptions, movie synopses) clearly labeled

## Editing Checklist
When editing a draft:
1. Does the opening hook immediately? (No throat-clearing — one sentence and we're in)
2. Would Luke say this out loud to a friend? (If not, rewrite it)
3. Is every paragraph earning its place? (Cut the filler)
4. Are there any AI-slop phrases? (Kill them — check the banned list)
5. Is the "not just X, Y" comma pattern anywhere? (Nuke it)
6. Does it have a clear point of view? (Wishy-washy = boring)
7. Are complex ideas translated into plain English immediately after being stated?
8. Are there vivid, grounded analogies (not corporate metaphors)?
9. Does it use the colon-label structure where appropriate?
10. Are sources/links woven in naturally?
11. Do the parallel sentence structures land? (Rhythm check — read out loud)
12. Is data presented as narrative, not just numbers?
13. Does the ending land with optimism and agency? (No "in conclusion")
14. **Final sniff test**: Does any sentence sound like ChatGPT wrote it? If yes, rewrite or cut.
