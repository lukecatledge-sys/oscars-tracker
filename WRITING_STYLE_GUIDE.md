# Luke Catledge — Substack Writing Style Guide

## Voice & Influences
- **Tim Ferriss-inspired**: Tactical, direct, structured but conversational. Breaks complex ideas into digestible frameworks.
- **Packy McCormick (Not Boring)**: Ambitious scope, connecting unexpected dots, making dense topics fun and accessible. Narrative-driven analysis.
- **Steve Jobs (hint of)**: Simplicity in explanation. Making the complex feel obvious. "Here's why this matters" clarity.
- **Overall tone**: Authentic, genuine, human. Crisp and clear but never robotic. Like talking to a sharp friend over coffee.

## Hard Rules — NEVER Do This
1. **NEVER use the "not just X, Y" comma correction pattern.** This is the #1 AI-slop tell. Examples of what to AVOID:
   - "It's a platform, not just a tool"
   - "This is transformative, not merely incremental"
   - "They're building a movement, not just a company"
   - Any variation where a comma introduces a correction/elevation of the first statement
2. **No AI-slop phrasing.** Kill these on sight:
   - "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
   - "Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this"
   - "At its core..."
   - "It's worth noting that..."
   - "The reality is..."
   - "Here's the thing..."
   - "Buckle up"
   - "Game-changer" / "paradigm shift"
   - "Delve" / "delve into"
   - "Leverage" (as a verb, unless quoting someone)
   - "Robust" / "Seamless" / "Synergy"
   - "Tapestry" / "Landscape" / "Realm"
   - "Navigate" (metaphorically)
   - "Foster" / "Foster innovation"
   - "In conclusion" / "To sum up"
   - "Without further ado"
   - "Think about it" (as a transition)
3. **No over-hedging.** Don't say "arguably" or "perhaps" every other sentence. Take a stance.
4. **No listicle energy** unless the format genuinely calls for it. Narrative > bullet points when telling a story.

## DO This — Style Principles
- **Be direct.** Lead with the interesting thing. Don't warm up for three paragraphs.
- **Short paragraphs.** White space is your friend. One idea per paragraph.
- **Conversational rhythm.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones. Read it out loud — it should sound like you talking.
- **Specificity over generality.** Names, numbers, dates, anecdotes > vague claims.
- **Show don't tell.** Instead of "this company is innovative," show what they did that was innovative.
- **Natural transitions.** No formulaic connectors. Let ideas flow the way they would in conversation.
- **Use analogies and metaphors** that feel grounded and original, not corporate-speak.
- **Bold claims, backed up.** Have a point of view. Then support it.

## Formatting Preferences
- Hyperlinks woven naturally into text (not dumped at the bottom)
- Bold for emphasis on key phrases sparingly
- Section headers when the piece is long enough to need them
- Pull quotes or callouts for particularly punchy lines (when the platform supports it)

## Editing Checklist
When editing a draft:
1. Does the opening hook immediately? (No throat-clearing)
2. Would I say this out loud to a friend? (If not, rewrite it)
3. Is every paragraph earning its place? (Cut the filler)
4. Are there any AI-slop phrases? (Kill them)
5. Does it have a clear point of view? (Wishy-washy = boring)
6. Are sources/links woven in naturally?
7. Does the ending land? (No "in conclusion" — end with a bang or a thought that lingers)
