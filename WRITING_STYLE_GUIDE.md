# Luke Catledge — Substack Writing Style Guide

## The Newsletter: "Recs"
- **Format**: Weekly recommendations — curated sections covering tools, movies, books, tech/macro trends, and quotes.
- **Tagline**: "Weekly recommendations of only the best for only the best"
- **Sign-off**: "All the love, all the power — Luke"
- **Listening note**: Often closes with "This 'Recs' was written while listening to [playlist]"

## Voice & Influences
- **Tim Ferriss**: The category-based "5-Bullet Friday" structure. Tactical, direct, structured but conversational. Breaks complex ideas into digestible frameworks.
- **Packy McCormick (Not Boring)**: Ambitious scope, connecting unexpected dots (AI hardware economics to Roman Emperor Hadrian in the same post). Making dense topics fun and accessible.
- **Steve Jobs (hint of)**: Simplicity in explanation. Takes the technical thing, then *immediately* translates it into plain English. "Inference (the AI talking back to you)." Making the complex feel obvious.
- **Overall tone**: Like texting a sharp friend who reads a lot. Warm, direct, curious. Never lecturing.

## Hard Rules — NEVER Do This
1. **NEVER use the "not just X, Y" comma correction pattern.** This is the #1 AI-slop tell. Examples of what to AVOID:
   - "It's a platform, not just a tool"
   - "This is transformative, not merely incremental"
   - "They're building a movement, not just a company"
   - Any variation where a comma introduces a correction/elevation of the first statement
2. **No AI-slop phrasing.** Kill these on sight:
   - "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
   - "Let's dive in" / "Let's unpack this"
   - "At its core..."
   - "It's worth noting that..."
   - "The reality is..."
   - "Here's the thing..."
   - "Buckle up"
   - "Game-changer" / "paradigm shift"
   - "Delve" / "delve into"
   - "Leverage" (as a verb, unless quoting someone)
   - "Robust" / "Seamless" / "Synergy"
   - "Tapestry" / "Landscape" / "Realm"
   - "Navigate" (metaphorically)
   - "Foster" / "Foster innovation"
   - "In conclusion" / "To sum up"
   - "Without further ado"
   - "Think about it" (as a transition)
   - "Revolutionize" / "Groundbreaking"
   - "It begs the question"
   - "In a world where..."
3. **No over-hedging.** Don't say "arguably" or "perhaps" every other sentence. Take a stance.
4. **No listicle energy** unless the format genuinely calls for it. Narrative > bullet points when telling a story.
5. **Never sound like a press release or a LinkedIn post.** No corporate-speak. No "excited to announce."

## Luke's Actual Patterns (from real posts)

### Opening Style
- Casual, warm, zero waste. One or two sentences max.
- Addresses the reader directly: "Hey crew:", "Hey friends"
- Often acknowledges a milestone or sets context in a single breath, then moves on: "we made it to number 50. I usually spend these milestones looking back, but this week feels more like a starting line. Let's get into it."

### Section Structure
- **Category headers** in a consistent format: "Tool I'm Experimenting With:", "Movie I'm Watching:", "Book I'm Reading:", "Quote I'm Pondering"
- Each section is a self-contained mini-essay (3-6 short paragraphs)
- Readers can skip around — each section earns its own attention

### How Luke Explains Complex Things
- State the technical concept, then **immediately parenthetical-translate** it:
  - "inference (the AI talking back to you)"
  - "saeculums" → then explains the cycle in plain terms
- Use grounded, vivid analogies:
  - "makes GPT-4 look like a pocket calculator"
  - "Just as a forest fire clears the brush"
  - "It's the 'Winter' of history"
- The "What does that actually mean for us?" pivot — goes from macro/technical to personal/practical

### Colon-Heavy Labeling
- Luke uses **bold colons** as structural scaffolding throughout:
  - "The Premise:", "The TL;DR:", "The Economics:", "The 2026 Reality:"
  - "Destruction is a Feature:", "High Stakes:"
- This is a signature move. It gives the reader a roadmap without feeling like a listicle.

### Sentence Rhythm
- Short declarative punches alternating with medium-length explanatory sentences:
  - "I played with it for an hour and shipped three projects. The speed is almost disorienting."
  - "I absolutely loved it." (Full stop. Conviction.)
  - "This is an early contender for the top three movies I'll watch in 2026."
- Average paragraph: 2-4 sentences. Rarely more.

### Conviction & Stance
- Takes clear positions without hedging:
  - "I absolutely loved it"
  - "this is the one to try"
  - "I've become obsessed with"
- When presenting others' ideas, still frames them through his own lens: "There's a strong argument to be made that..."

### Curation & Letting Others Speak
- Pulls in user reviews, block quotes, and other thinkers' words and lets them land
- Names his sources (Gavin Baker, Strauss & Howe, John Mackey) — gives credit, builds trust
- The "Reviews:" pattern: drops in 2-3 powerful quotes from real people to make the emotional case

### Connecting Unexpected Dots
- Draws lines between disparate topics in the same post (Hadrian's Rome → modern institutional decay → AI as "Radical Abundance")
- This is the Packy McCormick influence — the fun is in the unexpected connections

## Formatting Preferences
- **Bold category headers** for each section
- **Bold colons** for sub-points within sections (e.g., "The Economics:")
- Hyperlinks woven naturally into text (not dumped at the bottom)
- Images/screenshots placed inline where they add context
- Block quotes for reviews and notable quotes
- Bold for emphasis on key phrases — used sparingly but deliberately
- Short paragraphs with generous white space

## Editing Checklist
When editing a draft:
1. Does the opening hook immediately? (No throat-clearing — one sentence and we're in)
2. Would Luke say this out loud to a friend? (If not, rewrite it)
3. Is every paragraph earning its place? (Cut the filler)
4. Are there any AI-slop phrases? (Kill them — check the banned list)
5. Does it have a clear point of view? (Wishy-washy = boring)
6. Are complex ideas translated into plain English immediately after being stated?
7. Are sources/links woven in naturally?
8. Does it use the colon-label structure where appropriate?
9. Are there vivid, grounded analogies (not corporate metaphors)?
10. Does the ending land? (No "in conclusion" — end with the quote, the sign-off, or a thought that lingers)
11. **Final sniff test**: Does any sentence sound like ChatGPT wrote it? If yes, rewrite or cut.
