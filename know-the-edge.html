The History — Know Where It Came From

▼
1978
FSA Created — Revenue Act of 1978
The Revenue Act of 1978 established Flexible Spending Accounts. Before this, employers could contribute to Health Reimbursement Arrangements (HRAs) but employees couldn't customize for their own health needs. FSAs gave employees pre-tax control over medical spending for the first time. This was a landmark moment — the first time ordinary workers could shield health dollars from income tax.
1978–2013
The Use-It-or-Lose-It Era (35 Years)
For 35 years, the original FSA rule was brutal: any unused funds were forfeited on December 31. This discouraged people from contributing more than they were certain they'd spend, severely limiting the program's reach and utility. Employers kept the forfeited funds — creating a perverse incentive against employee wellness spending.
1996
Archer MSA Experiment — HIPAA
The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) created Archer Medical Savings Accounts as a limited pilot program. Severely restricted: capped at 750,000 self-employed taxpayers. Only ~150,000 accounts were ever opened. Too complex, too limited. But it planted the seed for what would become HSAs — proving the concept that tax-advantaged health savings could work.
December 8, 2003
HSA Created — The Big Moment
The Medicare Prescription Drug, Improvement, and Modernization Act was signed by President George W. Bush, effective January 1, 2004. HSAs were designed to pair with High-Deductible Health Plans (HDHPs), giving individuals the powerful "triple tax advantage" that FSAs lacked: tax-deductible contributions, tax-free growth, AND tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses. Unlike FSAs, funds roll over indefinitely and can be invested. This was the foundational moment for everything Truemed does today.
2013
FSA Gets a Lifeline
IRS introduced two options to soften the use-it-or-lose-it rule:
(1) Carryover: Roll over up to 20% of max annual contribution ($610 in 2024, $680 in 2026) to the next year.
(2) Grace Period: Use remaining funds through mid-March (2.5 months after plan year end).
Plans must choose one or neither — cannot offer both.
2024
HSA Hits Critical Mass
39+ million HSA accounts in the U.S. Total assets reach $146.9 billion (19% year-over-year growth). Investment assets surge to $64 billion (38% YoY growth), signaling a shift from "savings account" to "wealth-building tool." 1 in 5 Americans in their 30s now owns an HSA.
July 4, 2025
One Big Beautiful Bill — The Earthquake
The biggest HSA expansion since 2003. See dedicated section below.

HSAs are governed by Internal Revenue Code Section 223. The IRS sets contribution limits annually via Revenue Procedure. Qualified medical expenses are defined under IRC Section 213(d): expenses that "diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, or prevent disease, or affect any structure or function of the body."

Why This Language Matters for Truemed

That exact legal phrase — "affect any structure or function of the body" — is what enables Letters of Medical Necessity (LMNs). A licensed provider determines that a product (fitness equipment, sleep tech, supplements) meets this standard for a specific individual's medical condition. This is the legal bridge between "wellness" and "medical expense."

The Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN)

The Key Mechanism for Truemed's Business

A formal clinical document explaining why a specific product/service is essential for treating, mitigating, or preventing a diagnosed medical condition.

  • Must include: Diagnosis, ICD-10 codes, treatment details, medical justification, provider credentials
  • Issued by: Licensed providers (MD, NP, PA, LPC)
  • Validity: Typically 12 months
  • The bridge: Without an LMN, gym equipment/sleep tech/fitness apps = NOT HSA-eligible. With an LMN establishing medical necessity for the individual, they CAN qualify under §213(d)

How Truemed's Process Works

  • Step 1: Customer completes health survey at checkout
  • Step 2: Licensed provider reviews survey and health information (1-2 days)
  • Step 3: If medically necessary, LMN is issued and HSA/FSA payment proceeds

This clinical pathway is hard to replicate — it requires a provider network, clinical workflows, and liability management that pure payment processors don't have.
